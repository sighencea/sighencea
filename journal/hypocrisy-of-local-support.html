[Philosophy]

THE HYPOCRISY OF LOCAL SUPPORT

>> You're not paying for the object. You're paying for the human who makes it.

Everyone says they want "local."
Everyone posts "support your artisans."
Everyone loves the romance of craft.

Until they see the price.

Then local becomes "overpriced."
Artisan becomes "rip-off."
Craft becomes "I saw a similar one on AliExpress for 29."

The truth is simple and brutal:

You cannot demand local craftsmanship
and expect Chinese prices.

You're not paying for the object.
You're paying for the human who makes it.

[01] THE SOCIAL MEDIA PARADOX

Modern culture is obsessed with optics.

People post photos in front of farmers' markets,
tag local artists in their stories,
and talk about "community."

They want the appearance of supporting local—
the aesthetic of it—
not the responsibility.

Supporting local isn't a slogan.
It is an economic choice.

You do not support local by clapping at craft fairs.
You support local when you look a craftsman in the eyes
and say:

"Yes. I understand your price. And I accept it."

Most people can't do it.

They want the artisan's soul,
but at Amazon's checkout speed.

[02] THE PRICE OF WORK

People see the object.
They don't see the hours.

A belt does not cost 200 because of the leather.
It costs 200 because of:

rent on the workshop
training over years
mistakes paid with real money
premium hides imported from Italy
brass hardware sourced from suppliers, not flea markets
waxed thread
tools that cost more than their car insurance
tax, VAT, and bureaucracy

Craft is not a hobby.
It is an economy of consequences.

When you buy from a human who knows your name,
you are buying their:

time
skill
risk
reputation

You are paying for a lifetime of failures that made this one piece possible.

Factories don't have reputations.
They have volumes.

[03] THE FACTORY FANTASY

People compare my work to a 39 mass-produced belt.

They say:

"It looks similar."

Of course it does.
Factories spend millions making trash appear premium.

Perfect edge paint.
Perfect stitching.
Perfect shiny logo.

Until the moment you use it.

Then the coating cracks.
The stitching unravels.
The core reveals itself: cardboard resin and glue.

You bought an illusion.

Factories don't compete on durability.
They compete on impatience.

[04] FAILURE CYCLES

Disposable products are designed to die.

Their life cycle is not:

Buy → Use → Protect

It is:

Buy → Break → Replace

That is how you get "loyal customers."

A wallet that lasts 3 years will be replaced 3 times in a decade.
A wallet that lasts 20 years will be replaced 0 times.

Guess which one is good for business?

Craft is the enemy of profit.
Longevity is bad for revenue.
Quality destroys consumption models.

That is why corporations hate artisans.
We break their loops.

[05] WAR STORY

A customer once told me:

"Your belts are too expensive. I can get one on Amazon for 28."

He bought it.
He sent me a photo of it 7 months later.

The edges had split.
The leather peeled like laminate flooring.
The holes stretched into ovals.
The buckle plate bent.

Then he wrote the real sentence:

"I guess it's okay for 28."

That is the psychology of cheap:
fragile expectations,
fragile outcomes,
fragile identity.

He didn't feel cheated by the belt.
He felt protected by the price.

He expected failure and received it.
And his brain felt safe.

But here is what he never understood:

The 28 belt cost him 7 months of irritation.
Not money.
Mental friction.

He replaced it.
Then replaced it again.

Then he ordered mine.
He never wrote back.
He never needed to.

Craft kills the feedback loop.

[06] THE CULT OF DISCOUNT

People want "ethical manufacturing."
They want "no sweatshops."
They want "small business."

They also want:

free shipping
instant delivery
30-day returns
no repair responsibility
no waiting period

They want a world where the artisan suffers—
so the customer can feel morally superior
without paying the cost.

Craft is not cheap because it is small.
Craft is expensive because it is human.

There is no global supply chain to absorb mistakes.
No overseas factory to ignore failures.
No marketing department to hide flaws.

If I ruin a piece,
I lose days of work.
Not a SKU.
Not a spreadsheet cell.
Days.

You are not paying for an object.
You are paying for the possibility of imperfection—
and the discipline required to overcome it.

[07] THE LOCAL PARADOX

The same people who chant:

"We must protect our heritage!"

walk past the artisan table
and buy from the chain store.

The same people who complain:

"Nothing lasts anymore!"

throw away a wallet
because they saw something newer online.

The same people who scream:

"Craft is dying!"

refuse to pay the price that keeps it alive.

They want local as a slogan,
not a transaction.

They want culture as a festival prop,
not a responsibility.

They want artisans as quaint lifestyle decorations,
not professionals.

[08] VALUE IS NOT IN THE OBJECT

A handmade belt is not a strip of leather.
It is:

hand-selected hide
hand-cut edges
hand-punched holes
hand-stitched seams
hours of sanding
waxed edges
burnished surfaces
custom fit
guaranteed integrity

Factories can imitate appearance.
They cannot imitate intention.

Real craft is slow violence against entropy.

You are not buying the belt.
You are buying the man who won't let that belt die.

[09] THE BRUTAL TRUTH

If you want luxury quality at AliExpress prices,
you do not want craft.

You want exploitation.

You want a person in another country,
paid a fraction of a living wage,
working with chemicals that destroy their lungs,
to make you something you can brag about for a week.

You want a slave,
hidden behind a delivery label.

That is the hypocrisy of "local support."

People want ethics without cost.
They want morality without sacrifice.
They want quality without patience.

That world does not exist.

[10] CLOSING STATEMENT

Local is not a hashtag.
Local is a conversation with a human being.

Craft is not a hobby.
Craft is a refusal to participate in disposability.

When you buy from me,
you are not buying a belt or a wallet.
You are buying a decade of care.

If you want cheap,
you can find it.
It will die quickly
and quietly.

If you want something that survives your impatience,
you will come here.

AND YOU WILL UNDERSTAND WHY "EXPENSIVE"
IS THE COST OF HONESTY.
